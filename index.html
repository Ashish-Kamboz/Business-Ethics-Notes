Unit I — Introduction to Business Ethics

Definition

Business Ethics means applying moral principles and ethical standards to business decisions, policies and practices. It helps a business decide what is right and wrong while dealing with customers, employees, shareholders, government and society.

Easy Language: In simple words, business ethics means doing business honestly, fairly and responsibly. It is not only about following law; it is also about doing what is morally right. Ethical businesses focus on long-term success, trust and social welfare instead of only short-term profit.

Detailed Explanation

Business ethics studies the moral side of business activities. A business may earn profit, but the method of earning profit should be fair. For example, a company should not mislead customers, exploit employees, hide product defects or damage the environment only to increase sales. In exam answers, write that business ethics is wider than law because many actions can be legal but still morally wrong.

Important Points

  • It is a branch of applied ethics used in business life.
  • It ensures fairness, transparency and accountability.
  • It protects the interest of all stakeholders.
  • It goes beyond legal compliance and includes moral responsibility.
  • It supports long-term sustainable growth.
Real-life example: A company that clearly mentions product ingredients, correct price and safety information without misleading customers is following business ethics.
Applied ethicsFairnessTransparencyAccountabilityStakeholdersLong-term success
Definition

Ethics are systematic rules or standards that guide conduct in a group, profession or organization. Morals are personal beliefs about right and wrong developed through family, culture, religion, education and society.

Easy Language: Ethics are like professional rules. Morals are like your personal inner values. In business, ethics gives a framework to solve moral conflicts.

Detailed Explanation

Ethics and morals are related but not exactly same. Ethics are formal standards accepted by a profession or organization, while morals come from personal conscience and social upbringing. In business, employees may have different personal morals, so organizations create ethical codes to bring consistency in decisions. This helps solve conflicts between personal interest and professional duty.

Definition

The need for business ethics arises because business decisions affect many stakeholders and must be conducted in a fair, transparent and socially responsible manner.

Easy Language: Without ethics, companies may cheat customers, exploit employees, damage the environment or manipulate accounts. Ethics makes business trustworthy and safe for society.

Detailed Explanation

Business ethics is required because business has power over customers, employees, investors and society. If companies act only for profit, they may use unfair advertisements, unsafe products, low wages or false accounts. Ethics protects stakeholders and creates a stable business environment. Long-term reputation is built when people believe the company is honest even in difficult situations.

Important Points

  • Ensures fair business practices and prevents cheating, false advertising and price manipulation.
  • Builds trust and credibility among customers, investors and society.
  • Protects consumer rights such as right to safety, right to information and right to choice.
  • Improves employee satisfaction through fair wages, safe conditions and equal opportunities.
  • Reduces legal risks, fines and reputation loss.
  • Supports long-term sustainability by balancing profit with social and environmental responsibility.
  • Fulfills social responsibility through CSR, education, healthcare and environmental support.
  • Maintains corporate image and goodwill.
Real-life example: A company that gives correct warranty, accurate product information and after-sales service protects consumer rights and builds trust.

7-Mark Answer Angle

Business ethics is needed because modern organizations interact with customers, employees, investors, government and society. It ensures fair business practices, builds trust, protects consumers, improves employee motivation, reduces legal risk and supports long-term sustainability. For example, truthful advertising and fair pricing protect consumers, while safe working conditions protect employees. Ethical companies also perform CSR activities and maintain a positive reputation. Therefore, business ethics is both a moral duty and a business necessity.

Each type of ethics works at a different level. Personal ethics controls individual behaviour, professional ethics controls conduct of professionals, corporate ethics controls the whole organization, social ethics focuses on society, legal ethics ensures compliance with law, and environmental ethics protects nature. A good business needs all these levels together.

Business ethics gives both moral and practical benefits. Customers trust ethical brands, employees feel safe and motivated, investors prefer transparent companies and society supports responsible organizations. Ethical practices also reduce legal cases and scandals. Therefore, ethics becomes a competitive advantage, not just a moral lecture.

Definition

Principles of business ethics are basic moral guidelines that help employees and organizations act fairly, honestly and responsibly.

Easy Language: These principles are the rules of good business behavior. They help a company succeed legally and ethically.

Detailed Explanation

Principles of business ethics act like a checklist for right conduct. Accountability tells employees to accept responsibility. Honesty prevents false claims. Transparency builds trust. Fairness prevents misuse of power. Respect for laws keeps the business safe. Environmental concern reminds companies that profit should not harm nature.

Important Points

  • Accountability: accept responsibility for actions and decisions.
  • Respect for others: respect dignity, rights, freedom and privacy of every person.
  • Leadership: leaders should set good examples through ethical decisions.
  • Honesty: speak truth and avoid false claims or selective omissions.
  • Compliance with rules: follow company policies, industry standards and laws.
  • Loyalty: protect confidential information and avoid conflicts of interest.
  • Environmental concern: reduce pollution, waste and carbon emissions.
  • Transparency: make relevant information and policies accessible.
  • Integrity: keep promises, meet deadlines and act consistently.
  • Fairness: treat everyone equally and never misuse power.
  • Respect for laws: obey all local, state and national laws.
Real-life example: If a product defect is found, an ethical company accepts responsibility, informs customers and recalls the product.

Definition

Business ethics is influenced by internal and external factors that shape how people and organizations make ethical or unethical decisions.

Easy Language: People do not become ethical automatically. Their values, leaders, company culture, laws and pressure from society affect their behavior.

Detailed Explanation

Ethical behaviour is shaped by both internal and external conditions. A person with strong values may act honestly, but a corrupt culture or pressure from seniors can still create problems. Similarly, strict laws, media pressure and customer awareness can force companies to behave responsibly. That is why ethical systems, leadership and policies are necessary.

Important Points

  • Individual factors: personal values, beliefs, upbringing and education.
  • Organizational culture: shared norms and values of the company.
  • Leadership and management behavior: employees follow top management.
  • Legal and regulatory environment: strict laws discourage fraud, bribery and exploitation.
  • Economic environment: competition and financial crisis may create unethical pressure.
  • Social and cultural environment: social values influence expectations from business.
  • Stakeholder pressure: customers, investors, media and NGOs can force responsible behavior.
  • Corporate policies and codes of conduct: clear policies reduce confusion and misconduct.
Real-life example: If management rewards ethical employees and punishes misconduct, the organization develops a culture of integrity.

Definition

Organisational Ethics is the system of moral values, principles and standards that guide the behavior of individuals and groups within an organization.

Easy Language: It decides how employees, managers and leaders should behave while performing duties. It makes business decisions legally correct, morally right and socially responsible.

Detailed Explanation

Organisational ethics creates the moral framework of a company. It explains what behaviour is acceptable and unacceptable for employees, managers and leaders. It includes written rules, company culture and leadership behaviour. When organisational ethics is strong, people know how to act in difficult situations without depending only on personal judgement.

Important Points

  • Includes codes of conduct, corporate policies, ethical leadership and organizational culture.
  • Promotes integrity and honesty.
  • Ensures fair treatment of employees.
  • Builds trust among stakeholders.
  • Prevents corruption and misconduct.
  • Maintains corporate reputation.
Real-life example: A company with a clear code of conduct against bribery, discrimination and fraud is practicing organisational ethics.
Code of conductCorporate policiesEthical leadershipCultureIntegrityTrust
Definition

Ethical Corporate Behaviour means the conduct of an organization in a responsible, honest, fair and transparent manner while dealing with all stakeholders.

Easy Language: It is the practical action of organisational ethics. Organisational ethics gives values; ethical corporate behaviour shows those values in daily business.

Detailed Explanation

Ethical corporate behaviour is organisational ethics in action. A company may write values on paper, but those values matter only when they are followed in real decisions. For example, being transparent in financial reports, treating employees fairly and accepting responsibility for product defects are signs of ethical corporate behaviour.

Important Points

  • Transparency: truthful information about finance, policies and activities.
  • Accountability: accepting responsibility for decisions and outcomes.
  • Fair treatment of employees: equal opportunity, fair wages and safe conditions.
  • Honesty in dealings: no false advertising, fraud, bribery or corruption.
  • CSR: contribution to community and environment.
  • Compliance with laws: follow taxation, labour, environment and governance laws.
Real-life example: If a product defect harms customers, an ethical company recalls the product and compensates customers.
TransparencyAccountabilityFairnessHonestyCSRCompliance
Definition

Development of ethical corporate behaviour is a continuous process through which an organization builds a moral culture using clear policies, leadership, training and control systems.

Easy Language: Ethical behaviour does not come automatically. It has to be built by leaders, rules, training and strict action against wrong practices.

Detailed Explanation

Ethical corporate behaviour develops through repeated practice. A company first creates a code of ethics, then trains employees, monitors behaviour through audits, protects whistleblowers and rewards honest conduct. Leaders play the most important role because employees usually copy the behaviour of top management.

Important Points

  • Establish a written code of ethics.
  • Top management must show ethical leadership.
  • Conduct ethical training and awareness programs.
  • Create strong internal control systems like audits and compliance committees.
  • Provide whistleblower protection so employees can report wrong acts safely.
  • Reward ethical behaviour through recognition and appraisal.
  • Enforce discipline for unethical conduct.
  • Promote ethical organizational culture based on fairness, transparency and respect.
Real-life example: An anonymous complaint system helps employees report corruption without fear of retaliation.

7-Mark Answer Angle

Ethical corporate behaviour can be developed through a written code of ethics, ethical leadership, training programs, internal controls, whistleblower protection, rewards for ethical behaviour, strict discipline for misconduct and a culture of fairness and transparency. These steps help employees understand expectations and make responsible decisions. For example, regular audits prevent manipulation of accounts, while anti-corruption training creates awareness. Thus, ethical corporate behaviour is a continuous organization-wide process.

Definition

Ethical Leadership is leading by example according to moral principles such as integrity, fairness, honesty, accountability and social responsibility.

Easy Language: An ethical leader does not only talk about values; they show values through actions. Employees follow the behaviour of leaders.

Detailed Explanation

Ethical leadership is important because leaders set the tone of the whole organization. If leaders take shortcuts, employees also justify unethical acts. But if leaders are honest, fair and accountable, employees feel pressure and motivation to behave ethically. Ethical leaders focus on both performance and values.

Important Points

  • Integrity: consistent honesty in words and actions.
  • Fairness: equal and just treatment for everyone.
  • Accountability: taking responsibility for decisions.
  • Transparency: open communication and truthful information.
  • Empathy: understanding and respecting others.
  • Humility: accepting mistakes and learning from them.
  • Role-model behaviour: setting ethical standards through conduct.
  • Social responsibility: considering impact on society.
Real-life example: A leader who refuses to use corruption for quick profit encourages employees to act honestly.

Definition

Morals are principles of right and wrong behaviour. Values are deeply held beliefs about what is important and guide decision-making.

Easy Language: Values shape morals. If a person values honesty, their moral action will be telling the truth.

Detailed Explanation

Morals and values are the foundation of ethical behaviour. Values tell what is important, and morals tell what is right or wrong. For example, if an organization values transparency, its moral practice will be honest reporting. In business, shared values create a culture where ethical decisions become natural.

Definition

Moral issues arise when a business decision affects others and raises questions about right or wrong behaviour, especially when profit conflicts with ethical responsibility.

Easy Language: When a company chooses between money and morality, a moral issue may arise.

Detailed Explanation

Moral issues arise when business actions affect people and society. Many moral issues come from the conflict between profit and responsibility. For example, false advertising may increase sales but harms consumers. Financial fraud may attract investors temporarily but destroys trust later.

Important Points

  • Bribery and corruption: offering money or gifts to influence decisions.
  • False advertising: misleading consumers about product quality or benefits.
  • Exploitation of employees: low wages, excessive hours or unsafe conditions.
  • Environmental pollution: damaging air, water or land.
  • Price manipulation: artificial shortages or unfair pricing.
  • Financial fraud: manipulating records to show false profits.
  • Workplace discrimination: unfair treatment based on gender, caste, religion, age or race.
  • Conflict of interest: personal interest interfering with professional duty.
Real-life example: A manager awarding a contract to a family member instead of the best supplier is a conflict of interest.

Definition

An ethical dilemma is a situation where a person must choose between two or more alternatives, and each option has both positive and negative moral consequences.

Easy Language: It is a difficult choice where there is no perfect answer. One ethical value may conflict with another.

Detailed Explanation

An ethical dilemma is more difficult than a normal ethical issue because both choices may have good and bad consequences. For example, laying off employees may save the company but harm families. Keeping everyone employed may protect workers but risk business failure. The decision-maker must balance values, stakeholders and long-term impact.

Important Points

  • Conflict of values: two moral values clash.
  • Difficult decision-making: no clear right or wrong answer.
  • Impact on stakeholders: decision affects employees, customers, investors or society.
  • Moral responsibility: decision-maker must consider ethical consequences.
  • Common dilemmas: profit vs social responsibility, truth vs confidentiality, personal interest vs professional duty, layoffs vs employee welfare.
Real-life example: A company may know a product has a defect. Revealing it may reduce sales, but hiding it may mislead customers.
Conflict of valuesStakeholdersMoral responsibilityProfit vs societyTruth vs confidentiality

Definition

Workplace Ethics refers to the moral principles, values and standards that guide the behaviour of employees and employers within an organization.

Easy Language: It tells people how to behave professionally with colleagues, customers and management. It creates discipline, respect, honesty and a positive work environment.

Detailed Explanation

Workplace ethics applies moral values to daily office behaviour. It includes punctuality, honesty, confidentiality, respect, fair treatment and responsibility. Good workplace ethics creates a positive environment where employees trust each other and focus on performance instead of politics, fear or unfairness.

Important Points

  • Ensures discipline and professionalism.
  • Promotes respect among employees.
  • Encourages fair and honest behaviour.
  • Creates a positive and safe work environment.
  • Maintains confidentiality and responsibility.
  • Improves trust, productivity and organizational performance.
Real-life example: Being punctual, honest in reporting work and treating colleagues respectfully are examples of workplace ethics.

Definition

Workplace ethical behaviour is influenced by leadership, culture, personal values, rewards, peers, policies, stress and legal rules.

Easy Language: Employees act ethically when leaders set good examples, policies are clear and ethical behaviour is rewarded.

Detailed Explanation

Workplace ethical behaviour depends on leadership, culture, personal values and pressure. If employees see unethical people being rewarded, they may also follow wrong practices. Clear policies, fair punishment and ethical role models help employees choose the right path even under stress.

Important Points

  • Leadership and management behaviour strongly influence employees.
  • Organizational culture can promote transparency or unethical shortcuts.
  • Personal values of employees affect honesty and integrity.
  • Reward and punishment system decides what behaviour is encouraged.
  • Peer influence may create pressure to act ethically or unethically.
  • Clear organizational policies and code of conduct reduce confusion.
  • Work stress and unrealistic targets may push employees toward shortcuts.
  • Legal and regulatory environment discourages misconduct.
Real-life example: If a manager manipulates reports, employees may also start doing the same. If the manager acts honestly, employees are encouraged to act ethically.

Definition

Ethical issues at workplace arise when actions affect others and raise questions about fairness, honesty, respect and responsibility.

Easy Language: These are daily workplace problems where employees or managers must choose the fair and honest path.

Detailed Explanation

Workplace ethical issues are practical problems faced in daily professional life. Conflict of interest, discrimination, harassment and false communication damage trust and morale. These issues also reduce productivity because employees feel unsafe or unfairly treated. Ethical workplaces solve these problems through clear policies and fair action.

Important Points

  • Business Relationships: trust and fairness with customers, suppliers and colleagues.
  • Conflict of Interest: personal interest interferes with professional responsibility.
  • Fairness and Honesty: equal treatment and truthful conduct.
  • Communication Ethics: clear, truthful and responsible communication.
  • Discrimination: unfair treatment based on gender, caste, religion, age or other personal characteristics.
  • Harassment: unwanted behaviour that creates a hostile or uncomfortable workplace.
Real-life example: Promoting an employee because of personal friendship instead of merit is unfair and unethical.

Business ethics helps build a good society by ensuring responsible conduct, fairness, consumer protection, employee welfare, environmental protection and social welfare.

Business ethics helps society because companies affect jobs, prices, products, environment and public trust. Ethical companies protect consumers, provide fair wages, reduce pollution and support community welfare. When businesses avoid corruption and exploitation, the whole economic system becomes more trustworthy.

Ethical businesses build public trust and support sustainable development by balancing profit, social welfare and environmental protection.

Definition

Ethical issues in functional areas refer to moral problems or challenges that arise in different departments such as marketing, finance, HR, production, IT and gender-related workplace decisions.

Easy Language: Every department can face ethical problems because each department makes decisions that affect stakeholders.

Detailed Explanation

Functional area ethics means applying ethics separately in each department because every department has different risks. Marketing may mislead customers, finance may manipulate accounts, HR may discriminate, production may ignore safety, IT may misuse data and gender ethics may be violated through unequal treatment.

Important Points

  • Common concerns include misuse of power, lack of transparency, unfair practices and conflict between profit and ethics.
  • Ethical conduct in all departments maintains integrity, fairness and accountability.
  • Functional area ethics helps organizations avoid fraud, discrimination, unsafe products and misuse of data.
Real-life example: A marketing team making false claims, a finance team showing false profits, or an HR team discriminating in hiring are functional area ethical issues.

Definition

Marketing Ethics means applying moral principles to product promotion, pricing, advertising, customer communication and selling activities.

Easy Language: Marketing should not mislead customers. It should give correct information and protect consumer interests.

Detailed Explanation

Marketing ethics protects consumers from manipulation. Advertisements should be truthful, pricing should be fair and product information should be accurate. A company must not exaggerate benefits, hide side effects, create fake scarcity or use emotional pressure on vulnerable consumers.

Important Points

  • Avoid misleading advertisements and exaggerated claims.
  • Avoid false claims about product quality, features or benefits.
  • Avoid overpricing, hidden charges and price discrimination.
  • Do not sell harmful, unsafe or low-quality products.
  • Do not exploit vulnerable groups such as children or elderly customers.
  • Respect consumer privacy and avoid misuse of customer data.
Real-life example: A company exaggerating product benefits in advertisements to attract customers is unethical marketing.

Financial Ethics means applying honesty, transparency and responsibility in managing funds, investments, accounting and financial reporting.

Easy Language: Finance ethics prevents fraud and protects investors, banks and stakeholders from false information.

Detailed Explanation

Finance ethics is very important because financial information influences investors, banks, employees and government. False accounts, insider trading, misuse of company funds and tax evasion may give short-term gain but can lead to scandals, penalties and loss of public trust.

Important Points

  • Do not manipulate financial statements.
  • Do not show false profits to attract investors.
  • Avoid insider trading and misuse of confidential information.
  • Avoid tax evasion.
  • Do not misuse company funds.
  • Provide accurate and transparent financial reports.
Real-life example: Showing false profits to attract investors is unethical and illegal.

Production ethics focuses on product quality, safety and environmental responsibility. A company should not use cheap substandard material or skip safety checks to reduce cost. Ethical production ensures that products are safe for consumers and manufacturing does not harm workers or nature.

Definition

IT Ethics means responsible and fair use of technology, data and information systems while protecting privacy, security and intellectual property.

Easy Language: Organizations store huge amounts of data. Using or selling data without permission, hacking or misusing systems is unethical.

Detailed Explanation

IT ethics is becoming important because businesses collect and process large amounts of data. Misusing customer data, weak cybersecurity, hacking, piracy and biased algorithms can harm people. Ethical IT practices protect privacy, security and digital rights.

Important Points

  • Ensure data security against cyberattacks and breaches.
  • Avoid cybercrime such as hacking, phishing and identity theft.
  • Respect intellectual property; do not use pirated software or stolen digital content.
  • Prevent misuse of technology and unauthorized access.
  • Avoid excessive employee or user surveillance.
  • Check algorithmic or AI bias that may harm groups unfairly.
Real-life example: Selling customer data to advertisers without consent is an IT ethics violation.

Definition

Gender Ethics means ensuring fair, equal and respectful treatment of all genders in society and the workplace.

Easy Language: No person should be treated unfairly because of gender. Gender ethics promotes equality, dignity, safety and equal opportunities.

Detailed Explanation

Gender ethics ensures that all people get equal dignity, opportunity and safety regardless of gender. It includes equal pay for equal work, fair promotions, maternity support, prevention of harassment and respectful workplace culture. Gender equality improves both justice and organizational performance.

Important Points

  • Promote gender equality in hiring, pay, promotion and leadership.
  • Remove gender discrimination and unconscious bias.
  • Ensure safe and respectful workplace for all genders.
  • Prevent sexual harassment and gender-based harassment.
  • Provide equal employment opportunities.
  • Respect human dignity and rights.
  • Support work-life balance and inclusive policies.
